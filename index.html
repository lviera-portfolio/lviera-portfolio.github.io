The Dangers of Social Engineering

Context: Research paper written for CIS-199, Spring 2026. I chose this topic because I'm interested in educating people on social engineering and how AI can and will shape the future of attacks.

In the modern digital landscape, there is nothing more important in the world today than data. Whether it be your personal data, a company's corporate data, or highly classified government information, it doesn’t matter what category your data fits into because everybody wants their hands on your data and some will use social engineering to get it from you. One of the most common methods of extracting information from people is social engineering, being that social engineering attacks make up roughly ninety percent of all cyber-attacks, regardless of that it is still an underestimated form of cyberattack. Social engineering is defined by the US Department of State as, “the art of manipulating individuals to divulge confidential information or perform actions that compromise security.”. As stated, those who utilize social engineering techniques to target the biggest vulnerability of any security system which is the people within the organization. There are different social engineering attacks that these threat actors use to get information regarding the system such as: phishing, vishing, smishing, tailgating, whaling, etc... These bad actors during these social engineering attacks also weaponize human psychology by using certain principles that are designed to pressure the victim into giving up information the attacker is looking for. Therefore, social engineering training is a vital component in training new employees that should go further in depth as with the growth of AI only exacerbating the problem, we must put an end underestimating the threat that is social engineering attacks.

Social engineering attacks make up ninety percent of all cyber-attacks globally today; however not all social engineering attacks are the same, nor are the methodologies of performing these attacks. As previously stated, there are a multitude of different attacks such as: phishing, vishing, smishing, spear-phishing, and whaling, the most infamous technique among them being Phishing. Phishing attacks use email posing as a trustworthy organization I.E bank or work to try to get you to give up some sort of information, typically username and passwords to said account. Phishing attacks are so prevalent that it has other similar attacks that are named after it, such as Vishing, Smishing, & Spear-Phishing. Vishing is a similar attack to that of phishing, however instead of the form of contact with the victim being emailed, it's instead a phone call, and Smishing also functions similarly to the prior two attacks, but this attack weaponizes SMS/messenger apps. Spear-Phishing is like phishing attacks, although instead of being a generic phishing message that could’ve been sent to anyone it is specifically tailored to the recipient, including things like your name and using the name of a specific company to seem more credible. The methods of which these threat actors employ to manipulate the target all make use of psychology, they weaponize factors such as, Authority: posing as your boss/someone higher up than you to pressure you into doing what they want, Time/Urgency: using a sense of urgency to pressure you into making a decision quickly, relying on you making a mistake, Consensus: tries to influence your decision to do what they want by using a fake example of a previous encounter they had, Intimidation: tries to intimidate or scare you into doing what they want you to do. Some examples include, getting a text message from the RMV saying that you didn’t pay a toll and if you don't pay it within the next twenty-four hours your license will be revoked (Smishing and Intimidation), getting a phone call from a “customer” demanding that you change their password because last time they called, they changed their password no questions asked(Vishing and Consensus). Social engineering attacks thrive off human emotion, rather than technical vulnerabilities, making it one of the best tools in a threat actor’s arsenal. Understanding these tactics is the first step in recognizing and resisting them, before any damage can occur.

Proper training within organizations regarding social engineering attacks is vital to preventing data breaches, ensuring security within the company, by association also keeping your own data safe. While companies do train their employees on the dangers of social engineering attacks and how they work, they aren’t being properly trained as to how important the human factor is regarding social engineering attacks. Especially in smaller companies, “If an attack does occur, measures are created after the fact, i.e., when the attack happens. If the problem doesn't occur, it doesn't solve it, and they do not focus on prevention. They perceive the threats associated with social engineering as not very real.” (11. Tomsu, Rosfkova, Madlenak). Regardless of how big or small any company is everybody is still susceptible to cyber-attacks, especially in the modern world where attacks are getting more advanced by the minute, social engineering attack training should be at the forefront of security training because if you don't focus on the prevention of attacks and instead only fix them after you’ve already been breached doesn’t fix anything. Further, enabling a part of the problem, focusing on the repairing systems after a breach instead of taking the necessary steps to prevent the breaches in the first place. However, the way to remedy this problem is to reevaluate the way we train employees in social engineering and further reinforce what is being taught. Fortunately, there is a five-step process to better educate against these attacks, “1. Create a culture of security awareness, 2. Learn to recognize social engineering tactics, 3.Conduct regular phishing testing, 4. Complete regular patching and security updates, 5. Implement continuous monitoring” (8 Bonnie). To go more in depth the first step means to create a work environment that incorporates security into every facet of every position not to just leave security training to just IT but for everyone to be an active participant in it, and when new hires come in to make social engineering training an important part of training. The second step of this process is to teach employees what signs to lookout for, like double checking email addresses, not clicking suspicious links or files, and the third part of the process is to conduct regular phishing, to ascertain how effect the teams response is and to see what areas of security they need to work on. Step four doesn’t need that much explanation, just keep all software up to date with the newest patch, and step five is to monitor the web traffic to ensure that there is nothing suspicious going on without anybody noticing. Even this simple five step process is enough to prevent future cyber-attacks for whatever organization you represent because as costly as preventative measures can be for organizations, they keep out threat actors trying to gain access, especially since now that the team is knowledgeable about their security they are harder to trick. While reactionary security may be cheaper in the short-term, eventually it will all catchup to you because having to fix a new problem every so often will put unnecessary strain on your IT department and with the ever advancing threat of artificial intelligence already making social engineering attacks more difficult to prevent and more prevalent than ever, having the proper security training makes a world of difference.

Ever since AI became a common place thing over the past few years, a lot has changed in the realm of cyber-attacks, particularly social engineering attacks. Regarding social engineering attacks, threat actors have already started incorporating AI into their attacks, according to a study by Tech Advisors, “phishing attacks are skyrocketing--phishing surged 202% in late 2024, and over 80% of phishing emails now incorporate AI, with nearly 80% of recipients opening them. These messages are bypassing filters and fooling employees.” (2 Nat. Law Review). With phishing attacks on the rise because of AI making, it faster and easier to launch these attacks, combined with the fact that AI continues to improve further. With eighty percent of phishing emails having been opened and fooling said employees, AI has already reached the point where it is hard to distinguish between phishing attempts and real emails. Since AI in the cyber security space is not slowing down, this rapid innovation in cyber-attacks is already prompting security experts to evolve their tactics. They have started using AI to combat the bad actors with AI by using AI to filter through emails looking for inconsistencies, and similar behavior among differing emails. In the years to come following these massive developments regarding AI in social engineering attacks, as AI continues to improve and adapt, so will the attacks along with it, at the same time so will the technology that security experts have designed to keep it in check, but as it stands in this present day AI is going to be a massive threat to security.

In conclusion, social engineering is the most important factor of cyber-attacks out there, making up ninety percent of all cyber-attacks globally, therefore training for social engineering should be of the utmost importance. However, even though it makes up a majority of attacks in the world, it is still an underestimated attack method because even though there is training for these attacks, the training doesn’t cover the most vital component of said attacks, the human factor. They train people to be warry of emails, however they don’t go in depth enough as to how these attacks use your own psychology against you to manipulate you into giving them what they want, like using the fear of authority/getting in trouble, or wanting to fit in/consensus. It could be improved by providing expansive and detailed training to the company as a whole in order to prevent attacks organization wide. In addition to that, with the recent boom in the AI industry effecting every other industry cybersecurity is no different, as threat actors have already started using it to empower phishing attacks, making up eighty percent of phishing attacks according to Tech Advisors. As AI continues to grow, it will get harder for organizations and your average man to tell what a phishing attack is and what isn’t one. Making learning about what signs to look for in social engineering attacks, knowing about the different methodologies of attacks, the human psychology that goes into it, how to protect yourself against it is vital, when living in a world where everyone wants your data.
